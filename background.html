Background

Two Visions of Conservation

By the early 1900s, the conservation movement had split into two competing visions. Preservationists, led by John Muir, argued that natural landscapes had intrinsic value and should remain untouched. Conservationists, led by Gifford Pinchot, believed that natural resources should be managed and used for the benefit of the public. Under President Theodore Roosevelt, both ideas had coexisted, with Roosevelt focusing on their agreements, which expanded federal involvement in land management while also establishing national parks. However, Hetch Hetchy exposed the tension within that system.

Conservationists argued that by flooding Hetch Hetchy they would build San Francisco a massive water supply to support the city and the broader area, while preservationists considered it sacred and wanted no human development at all.

"Dam Hetch Hetchy! As well dam for water-tanks the people's cathedrals and churches, for no holier temple has ever been consecrated by the heart of man."

— John Muir, The Yosemite, 1912

The Two Figures

Gifford Pinchot

Yale-educated and trained in scientific forestry, Pinchot became the first Chief Forester under Theodore Roosevelt. He built the U.S. Forest Service around a single principle: the greatest good for the greatest number in the long run. For Pinchot, conservation meant use; resources existed to serve public needs.

John Muir

John Muir, founder of the Sierra Club, approached nature as something sacred. To him, places like Hetch Hetchy were not resources to be managed, but landscapes to be preserved in their natural state. His arguments were grounded in intrinsic value and spiritual significance, a perspective that resonated strongly with the public.

Together, Muir and Pinchot represented competing models for how the United States should define and govern its natural environment, a conflict that Congress would ultimately have to resolve.

The 1906 Earthquake and the Road to Congress

When the earthquake struck San Francisco in April 1906, fires burned for three days as water pressure failed across the city. In the aftermath, securing a reliable water supply became a matter of survival. Hetch Hetchy Valley, already identified as a potential source, took on new urgency.

What had been an ongoing dispute over land use escalated into a national issue shaped by disaster, urban growth, and political pressure. For over a decade, attempts to dam the valley had been blocked by officials who viewed flooding a national park as an overreach of federal authority. But by 1913, conditions had shifted.

On December 1, 1913, Gifford Pinchot appeared before Congress in this context. His goal was to secure agreement from lawmakers to flood the valley, even though Congress was facing a controversial decision that would shape how the government approached environmental policy.
