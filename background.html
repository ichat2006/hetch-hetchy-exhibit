Background

This was the only time in United States history that Congress voted to flood a national park. The decision over Hetch Hetchy Valley forced lawmakers to determine whether protected land could be altered when public need appeared great enough, setting a precedent for how the federal government would engage with the environment going forward.

Two Visions of Conservation

At the center of the Hetch Hetchy debate was a single, unprecedented decision: whether a valley inside a federally protected national park could be permanently flooded to supply water to a growing city. The question forced Congress to confront not just a policy choice, but the limits of its authority over protected land.

By the early 1900s, the conservation movement had split into two competing visions. Preservationists, led by John Muir, argued that natural landscapes had intrinsic value and should remain untouched. Conservationists, led by Gifford Pinchot, believed that natural resources should be managed and used for the benefit of the public. Under President Theodore Roosevelt, both ideas had coexisted within the broader Progressive movement, which expanded federal involvement in land management while also establishing national parks. Hetch Hetchy exposed the tension within that system. Protecting land and using it for public benefit were no longer compatible goals.

This is what made the issue controversial. Flooding Hetch Hetchy was not simply a local decision, it challenged the meaning of federal conservation policy itself. If a national park could be altered for utility, then protection was no longer absolute. The outcome would determine whether government responsibility meant preserving nature from use, or managing it in the name of the public good.

"Dam Hetch Hetchy! As well dam for water-tanks the people's cathedrals and churches, for no holier temple has ever been consecrated by the heart of man."

— John Muir, The Yosemite, 1912

The Two Figures

Gifford Pinchot

Yale-educated and trained in scientific forestry, Pinchot became the first Chief Forester under Theodore Roosevelt. He built the U.S. Forest Service around a single principle: the greatest good for the greatest number in the long run. For Pinchot, conservation meant use, resources existed to serve public needs. By 1913, even after leaving office, his authority and influence remained central to how policymakers understood conservation.

John Muir

John Muir, founder of the Sierra Club, approached nature as something sacred. To him, places like Hetch Hetchy were not resources to be managed, but landscapes to be preserved in their natural state. His arguments were grounded in intrinsic value and spiritual significance, a perspective that resonated with the public but proved difficult to translate into the language of policy.

Together, Muir and Pinchot represented competing models for how the United States should define and govern its natural environment, a conflict that Congress would ultimately have to resolve.

The 1906 Earthquake and the Road to Congress

When the earthquake struck San Francisco in April 1906, fires burned for three days as water pressure failed across the city. More than 25,000 buildings were destroyed. In the aftermath, securing a reliable water supply became a matter of survival. Hetch Hetchy Valley, already identified as a potential source, took on new urgency.

What had been an ongoing dispute over land use escalated into a national issue shaped by disaster, urban growth, and political pressure. For over a decade, attempts to dam the valley had been blocked by officials who viewed flooding a national park as an overreach of federal authority. But by 1913, conditions had shifted. Progressive Era values emphasized efficiency, expertise, and large-scale solutions, making arguments based on public necessity more compelling.

By the time the issue reached Congress, lawmakers were not simply weighing environmental values. They were deciding whether the federal government should act to meet the needs of a growing population, even if that meant redefining the limits of protected land. The decision carried political risk and long-term consequences, as it would establish a precedent for future federal actions involving the environment.

On December 1, 1913, Gifford Pinchot appeared before Congress in this context. His goal was to secure agreement from lawmakers facing a controversial decision that would shape how the government approached environmental policy in the years to come.
