Background

Two Visions of Conservation

By the early 1900s, the conservation movement had split into two competing visions.6 Preservationists, led by John Muir, argued that natural landscapes had intrinsic value and should remain untouched. Conservationists, led by Gifford Pinchot, believed that natural resources should be managed and used for the benefit of the public.2 Under President Theodore Roosevelt, both ideas coexisted, expanding federal involvement in land management while also establishing national parks.2 However, Hetch Hetchy exposed the tension within that system.

Conservationists argued that flooding Hetch Hetchy would provide San Francisco with a reliable water supply and support future growth, while preservationists viewed the valley as sacred and opposed any development.7

"Dam Hetch Hetchy! As well dam for water-tanks the people's cathedrals and churches..."4

— John Muir, The Yosemite, 1912

The Two Figures

Gifford Pinchot

Trained in scientific forestry, Pinchot became the first Chief Forester under Theodore Roosevelt and helped establish the U.S. Forest Service.5 His approach emphasized efficiency and the idea of the “greatest good for the greatest number,” defining conservation as use rather than preservation.2

John Muir

Founder of the Sierra Club, Muir approached nature as something sacred and argued that landscapes should remain untouched.7 His perspective emphasized intrinsic value and spiritual meaning, which resonated strongly with the public.7

Together, Muir and Pinchot represented competing models for how the United States should define and govern its natural environment.6

The 1906 Earthquake and the Road to Congress

When the earthquake struck San Francisco in April 1906, fires burned for three days as water pressure failed across the city.6 In the aftermath, securing a reliable water supply became a matter of survival, pushing the search for a stable water source into the national spotlight.1

Hetch Hetchy Valley, already identified as a potential reservoir site, became central to that effort.1 What had previously been a regional dispute over land use escalated into a national issue shaped by disaster, urban expansion, and political pressure.6

For over a decade, attempts to dam the valley had been blocked due to concerns about federal overreach and the protection of national parks.7 By 1913, however, the urgency created by the earthquake and continued growth made the project increasingly difficult to oppose.6

On December 1, 1913, Gifford Pinchot appeared before Congress in this context. His goal was to secure agreement from lawmakers facing a politically risky decision that would set a precedent for how the federal government would approach environmental policy moving forward.3
