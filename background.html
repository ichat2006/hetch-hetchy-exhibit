Background

Before Pinchot walked into that hearing room, a conservation movement had split in two, a city had burned, and a decade of political maneuvering had set the stage for the most consequential environmental vote in American history.

Two Visions of Conservation

By the early 1900s, conservation in the United States had split into two sharply opposed camps. On one side, John Muir and fellow preservationists argued that nature had value in itself — it should stay wild and untouched. On the other, Gifford Pinchot and the conservationists held that nature should serve people; resources were meant to be used for the greater good.

To Muir, Hetch Hetchy Valley wasn't real estate. It was sacred — unique and irreplaceable, something he described as "a grand landscape garden, one of Nature's rarest and most precious mountain temples." Pinchot's stance was entirely different. He saw the valley as valuable only if it could meet human needs. For him, the measure of any resource was what it could do for the most people.

"Dam Hetch Hetchy! As well dam for water-tanks the people's cathedrals and churches, for no holier temple has ever been consecrated by the heart of man."

— John Muir, The Yosemite, 1912

The Two Figures

Gifford Pinchot

Yale-educated, politically connected, and trained in European scientific forestry, Pinchot became America's first Chief Forester in 1905 under Theodore Roosevelt. He built the U.S. Forest Service on a single governing principle borrowed from Jeremy Bentham's utilitarianism: the greatest good for the greatest number in the long run. For Pinchot, conservation meant wise use — not preservation for its own sake, but managed development that served the public.

By 1913 he had been fired by President Taft, but his authority as the intellectual godfather of American conservation was unmatched. When he walked into the hearing room, Congress knew exactly who he was.

John Muir

Scottish-born, self-taught, and spiritually driven, Muir spent decades living in the Sierra Nevada and became America's most beloved nature writer. He co-founded the Sierra Club in 1892 and led the campaign that established Yosemite National Park. For Muir, wild places had intrinsic value — they were not resources to be managed, but temples to be protected.

Muir did not testify before Congress in 1913. His argument was made in print — most powerfully in the final chapter of The Yosemite (1912). His language was explicitly sacred, and in a congressional hearing room calibrated to Progressive Era rationalism, that proved to be a structural disadvantage.

The 1906 Earthquake and the Road to Congress

When the earthquake struck San Francisco in April 1906, fires burned for three days. Water pressure failed. More than 25,000 buildings were destroyed. City leaders emerged from the wreckage with one obsession: San Francisco needed a new, reliable water supply that would survive the next disaster.

Hetch Hetchy Valley had already been targeted — former Mayor James Phelan had filed water rights claims on the Tuolumne River as early as 1902. After the earthquake, those claims gained the emotional force of survival. The debate ran from 1902 all the way to 1913, blocked repeatedly by Interior Secretaries who saw flooding a national park as an overreach.

Then Woodrow Wilson was elected. He appointed Franklin K. Lane — San Francisco's former city attorney — as Secretary of the Interior. The political window was finally open. All that remained was to make the public argument convincingly enough to pass the bill. That is what Gifford Pinchot came to Congress to do on December 1, 1913.
