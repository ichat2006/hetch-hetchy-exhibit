About This Exhibit

A Moment That Changed American Conservation

On December 1, 1913, Gifford Pinchot — the first Chief Forester of the United States — stepped before the House Committee on Public Lands and delivered a few hundred carefully chosen words. Those words helped authorize the flooding of Hetch Hetchy Valley, a landscape inside Yosemite National Park described by naturalist John Muir as a twin to Yosemite itself.

This exhibit examines Pinchot's testimony as a primary source in rhetorical analysis: who was speaking, to whom, about what, and how. It explores the clash of two philosophies — conservation-as-use vs. conservation-as-preservation — that continues to shape environmental debates to this day.

"The fundamental principle of the whole conservation policy is that of use — to take every part of the land and its resources and put it to that use in which it will best serve the most people." — Gifford Pinchot, Testimony Before Congress, December 1, 1913
