Analysis of Gifford Pinchot's 1913 Congressional Testimony about the Hetchy Hetchy Damn

Ishaan Chaturvedi  ·  English 1102  ·  Dr. Young  ·  April 23, 2026

About This Exhibit

In December 1913, Gifford Pinchot testified before Congress in support of the Raker Act — legislation that would authorize the flooding of Hetch Hetchy Valley inside Yosemite National Park. His testimony did not win by overwhelming the opposition with evidence. It won by changing the terms of the debate entirely, making only one conclusion seem reasonable before any real argument had been made.

This exhibit follows the structure of the rhetorical analysis essay: first establishing the historical background and the two figures at the center of the debate, then walking through each of Pinchot's rhetorical moves in the testimony itself, then examining what the outcome meant for American conservation — and still means today.

01 Background The conservation split, the 1906 earthquake, and how the Hetch Hetchy debate reached Congress. 02 The Testimony Close reading of Pinchot's December 1, 1913 testimony — each rhetorical move analyzed alongside the actual transcript. 03 Outcome & Legacy The vote, the dam, Muir's death, and why Pinchot's framework still shapes environmental debates today. 04 The Essay The full rhetorical analysis essay in one place.

"The fundamental principle of the whole conservation policy is that of use — to take every part of the land and its resources and put it to that use in which it will best serve the most people."

— Gifford Pinchot, Testimony Before Congress, December 1, 1913
