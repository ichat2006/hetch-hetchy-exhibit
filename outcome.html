Outcome & Legacy

The Vote

The House passed H.R. 7207 — the Raker Act — on December 7, 1913. The Senate followed. President Woodrow Wilson signed it into law on December 19, 1913. Construction of the O'Shaughnessy Dam began in 1914 and was completed in 1923. Hetch Hetchy Valley filled with water. It has remained submerged for over a century.

Why It Still Matters

Pinchot’s testimony did not just help pass the Raker Act; it shifted how environmental decisions are made at the federal level. By tying conservation to use and public benefit, it provided the government a framework they could use to justify environmental harm or endangerment.

As long as the government can provide an argument that a decision helps the public, it can justify damaging natural environments. For example, under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), federal agencies are required to evaluate environmental impacts before approving major projects. However, the law does not prevent those projects from happening. Instead, it allows projects to proceed if agencies determine that they do not have a “significant effect on the human environment.” This standard is often used by both companies and the government to move forward with projects while overlooking impacts on the surrounding ecosystem or the cultural and spiritual significance of a certain environment, reflecting a view of nature as something valuable only for what can be used or extracted from it.

This same logic appeared in the approval of the Dakota Access Pipeline. In 2016, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers granted the necessary permits after determining that the project would not be “injurious to the public interest.” Rather than focusing on the impacts to the surrounding ecosystem or the significance of the land to the Standing Rock Sioux people, the decision centered on perceived utility, scale, and broader benefit to the public.

In both cases, the structure of the decision follows the same pattern. Once decisions are framed in terms of impact thresholds, necessity, and public interest, certain outcomes become easier to justify than others. Arguments based on intrinsic value or cultural significance remain present, but they operate within a system that requires them to compete against quantified benefit. As a result, those arguments are often pushed aside in favor of decisions that prioritize use, reinforcing a system that views nature primarily as a resource rather than something with its own independent value.
