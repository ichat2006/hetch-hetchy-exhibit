Outcome & Legacy

The Vote

The House passed H.R. 7207 — the Raker Act — on December 7, 1913. The Senate followed. President Woodrow Wilson signed it into law on December 19, 1913.3 Construction of the O'Shaughnessy Dam began in 1914 and was completed in 1923. Hetch Hetchy Valley filled with water. It has remained submerged for over a century.

Why It Still Matters

Pinchot's testimony did not just help pass the Raker Act. It shifted how the federal government thinks about environmental decisions. By framing conservation as use in service of the public good, Pinchot gave policymakers a way of talking about nature that treated it as valuable mainly for what it could provide to people. Over time, that way of thinking became written into law.1

The National Environmental Policy Act of 1969 is the most direct example. NEPA requires federal agencies to study the environmental effects of major projects before approving them, but it does not give those studies the power to stop a project.4 A project can move forward as long as an agency determines it will not have a "significant effect on the human environment." That standard is built around human utility. Concerns about ecosystems or the cultural meaning of a place can be raised, but they have to make their case inside a process that was not designed to treat them as equal to economic or practical benefit.

The Dakota Access Pipeline is where that pattern becomes hard to overlook. The Standing Rock Sioux Tribe raised objections throughout the approval process. They argued the pipeline ran through land of cultural and spiritual importance to their community and that it threatened their water supply. In July 2016, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers concluded the project's effects were "not injurious to the public interest" and approved it.5, 6 The tribe's concerns were part of the record. They just did not fit the terms the process was built to evaluate. A federal court later found the Corps had not properly consulted with the tribe, which reflects how seriously those concerns were weighed.7

Pinchot did not write NEPA and he did not build the Dakota Access Pipeline. What connects them is the logic he helped make the default in American environmental policy. When decisions get framed around public necessity and measurable benefit, other kinds of arguments do not get dismissed outright. They get processed through a system that was not built to take them seriously. That system has a history, and it starts somewhere around a congressional hearing room in December 1913.1
