Outcome & Legacy

The Vote

The Raker Act passed in 1913, allowing the construction of the O'Shaughnessy Dam. Hetch Hetchy Valley was flooded, permanently altering the landscape.1

Why It Still Matters

Pinchot’s testimony did not just help pass the Raker Act — it reshaped how environmental decisions are made at the federal level. By tying conservation to use and public benefit, it provided the government a framework that could justify environmental harm as long as it served a broader purpose.

Under the National Environmental Policy Act, federal agencies evaluate environmental impact but allow projects to proceed if they do not have a “significant effect on the human environment.”2 This standard shifts focus away from nature itself and toward human-centered impact.

This same logic appeared in the Dakota Access Pipeline decision. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers determined the project would not be “injurious to the public interest,” weighing the “benefits… against its reasonably foreseeable detriments.”3

In both cases, decisions are structured around measurable benefit rather than intrinsic value. Nature is treated not as something to preserve, but as something to use, as long as the use can be justified.
